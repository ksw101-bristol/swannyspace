From Dusty Boots to Golden Gloves:
24 Games, 15 Pundits, Zero Mercy

As the group stage reaches its halfway point, the league table tells a story of shock leaders, spectacular collapses, and one cockney's redemption arc that nobody saw coming.

By Our Chief Correspondent · Swannyspace.com · June 2026
Round 1 — Games 1 to 4

The Creator Takes an Early Bow

It was supposed to be a quiet opening night for Kirk Walsh — league founder, reluctant protagonist, man who built the whole website and then immediately became the person everyone wants to beat. Mexico's 2–0 dismantling of South Africa handed Kirk a perfect 5pts on the very first match, a result he reportedly celebrated with visible embarrassment.

"He looked mortified," one insider told this correspondent. "Like he'd shown up to his own surprise party." The exact score was shared only by his brother-in-law Chris Hunt and the formidable Cathy & Oliver partnership — a mother-and-son duo who immediately announced themselves as forces to be reckoned with.

South Korea's 2–1 win over Czechia extended Kirk's misery of success — another 5pts, a score also nailed by the wily Lee May and the equally dangerous Jason Parsons, who to nobody's surprise had predicted exactly 2–1.

The Assassin Misfires. The Big Man Strikes.

Mike Budd — known in these parts as "The Assassin," a man whose very nickname suggests cold precision — opened his account with a measly 1 point from the first four games. Brutal. Clinical he was not.

Meanwhile his older brother Dave, a man who spends his working life hunting cybersecurity vulnerabilities, proved equally adept at hunting out correct scorelines. Dave sniffed out Canada's 1–1 draw with Bosnia, one of only three to do so alongside Steve Kenna and Lee Grainger.

Steve — known universally as "the Big Man," a gentle giant who regards his 10,000 daily steps with a reverence normally reserved for religious observance — stepped out of the gate with 9 points, establishing himself as an early contender. The steps, apparently, are working.

Cathy & Oliver: The Wildcard That Wasn't

The mother-son combination of Cathy & Oliver — making Parsons simultaneously a competitor and a man playing against his own family — stormed to the top of the Round 1 table with a remarkable 11 points.

Parsons himself, a man who treats the scoreline 2–1 the way other people treat oxygen — essential, ever-present, non-negotiable — could only watch as his own household outperformed him.

At the bottom of the pile: Richard Boyce, couch-surfing chairman of a football club, a man tall enough to see the game from further away than most, managed just 1 point. His brother James matched him exactly. Sibling solidarity, perhaps. Or shared tactical incompetence.

Round 2 — Games 5 to 12

Jordy's Resurrection. Kirk's Implosion.

If Round 1 belonged to the establishment, Round 2 was where the plot thickened spectacularly. Jordy Chalmers — a cockney of uncertain vintage — produced the round of the tournament so far, banking 11 points to storm from last place into contention.

His secret weapon? Netherlands 2–2 Japan — a result only Jordy and Chris Hunt called correctly, good for a precious 5pts apiece. Nobody saw that one coming. Least of all Jordy, one suspects, who may have been nursing certain consequences of a heavy evening when he entered his prediction.

Kirk Walsh, meanwhile, endured the kind of Round 2 that tests a man's faith in his own project. Three points. The creator, humbled by his own creation. The website worked perfectly. The predictions did not.

"Kirk Walsh — founder, frontrunner, and then, quite suddenly, also-ran. The universe, as ever, has a sense of humour."

Cameron Walsh — Kirk's son, a central defensive rock in temperament if not in points — posted a solid 9 in Round 2, suggesting the apple may not fall as far from the tree as Round 1 implied. His brother Elliot, a Chelsea fan still visibly mourning the absence of Cole Palmer from the England squad, also found his stride with 3 — modest, but the passion was there.

Germany 7, Curaçao 1: The Scoreline That Defeated Everyone

Germany's extraordinary 7–1 evisceration of Curaçao provided the moment of Round 2 — a scoreline so preposterous that nobody predicted it exactly, yet so emphatically one-sided that all 15 contestants at least got the direction right. One point each. The consolation prize of the group stage.

Scotland's 0–1 defeat to Haiti proved kinder to those willing to back the underdog. Lee May — the balding Alan Shearer lookalike whose football instincts run considerably deeper than his hairline — called it correctly, as did Cameron Walsh and James Boyce. Three points each, and the quiet satisfaction of having backed Haiti.

Georgia & Harry May — Lee's children, competing against their own father with the cheerful ruthlessness only offspring can muster — were having a quieter round. 2 points. Dad was ahead. For now.

Round 3 — Games 13 to 24

Georgia & Harry: The Next Generation Strikes

Round 3 belonged to the young guns. Georgia & Harry May — Lee's children, playing against their father in what must make for interesting dinner table conversation — posted a stunning 18 points to top the round. The defining moment: Belgium's 1–1 draw with Egypt, called exactly and exclusively by the next generation. 5pts while Dad watched on.

France's 3–1 win over Senegal was the round's defining scoreline — six people called it exactly: Mike Budd (the Assassin, finally assassinating something), Richard Boyce (the tall couch-surfing chairman, rising from the canvas), Jordy Chalmers, Steve Kenna, Lee Grainger, and Jason Parsons. Remarkably, it was not 2–1.

Spain 0–0 Cape Verde: The Great Humbling

It was the result that made everyone look foolish simultaneously. Spain's goalless draw with Cape Verde — a result so unexpected it briefly broke the scoreboard — yielded zero points for all 15 contestants. Every single one had backed Spain to win. Every single one was wrong.

Richard Boyce had called 4–0. Elliot Walsh had predicted 3–0. Even the more cautious souls among the group had Spain winning comfortably. Cape Verde had other ideas. This is, of course, why we watch football.

"Spain 0–0 Cape Verde. Fifteen predictions. Fifteen wrong. A moment of pure, democratic humiliation."

Portugal's 1–1 draw with Congo DR was another collective embarrassment — not a single contestant called it. Dave Budd, whose cybersecurity background makes him naturally suspicious of obvious answers, had at least hedged with a modest scoreline. It wasn't enough.

England 4–2 Croatia: Elliot's Finest Hour

Elliot Walsh had been carrying the weight of Cole Palmer's World Cup absence like a personal affront. But England's 4–2 dismantling of Croatia — a swashbuckling, irresponsible, wonderful scoreline — was vindication. He alone called Uzbekistan's 1–3 defeat to Colombia, banking a 5pts masterpiece at the death to close out a strong round.

Lee Grainger — another man of considerable stature, one who regards Oasis with the reverence most people reserve for religious texts — continued his steady accumulation, while Chris Hunt, Man United fan and Kirk's brother-in-law, showed that artistic sensibility translates surprisingly well to football prediction. 12 points in Round 3. The canvas, as it were, was filling nicely.

The Shearer Leads. The Creator Trails.

After 24 games, Lee May — the man who most resembles a bald, slightly-less-shouty Alan Shearer — sits at the summit on 29 points. Cool, composed, and quietly devastating, much like the real Shearer in his prime, only with less fanmail and more correct scorelines.

The remarkable thing about this leaderboard is its congestion. Four players on 28 points. Steve Kenna breathing down their necks on 27. Jordy Chalmers and Richard Boyce — the cockney and the chairman — tied on 25. After 24 games, this is anybody's competition.

The man with the most to contemplate is Kirk Walsh himself: 21 points, 12th place, a man watching the league he built from a position of respectful mediocrity. He started the tournament embarrassed by early success. Now he's merely embarrassed. Progress, of a kind.

Bottom of the pile: James Boyce on 16, whose brother Richard's revival in Round 3 will only intensify an already complicated sibling dynamic. The Assassin (Mike) sits just above on 21, which is frankly unacceptable for a man with such a menacing nickname.

Round 4 begins now. Twenty-four more games. The group stage concludes. And somewhere out there, Jordy Chalmers is either celebrating or sleeping it off. Possibly both.
